# How to Care for Lash Extensions: The Day-by-Day Aftercare Guide (Chiang Mai 2026)

*Published by My Lash House · Compiled by Ying, certified lash artist · Published June 26, 2026 · Last updated June 26, 2026*

*Disclosure: This guide is published by My Lash House, a private one-on-one lash studio in San Klang, Chiang Mai. It reflects the aftercare advice given to every client at the end of their appointment. Where standard industry practice differs from what's described here, the guide says so directly.*

To make lash extensions last 3–4 weeks, follow three rules: **avoid water and steam for the first 24 hours**, **never use oil-based products around the eye area**, and **brush the lashes daily with a clean spoolie.** Beyond that: sleep on your back when possible, skip lash curlers and waterproof mascara, and book a refill every 2–3 weeks.

This guide is the practical day-by-day handbook. Each section names what to do, what to avoid, and why — so the rules stick instead of feeling arbitrary.

## The short answer

The three things that actually move the retention dial: **(1)** keep lashes dry for 24 hours so the cyanoacrylate adhesive can fully cure, **(2)** avoid oil-based skincare and makeup near the eye area indefinitely (oil dissolves the adhesive bond), and **(3)** clean daily with a foam lash shampoo and brush with a spoolie. The rest — sleep position, mascara, eyelash curlers, refill cadence — matters at the margins. Follow the rules and a properly applied set lasts a full 3–4 weeks. Skip them and the set halves in lifespan regardless of application quality.

## The first 24 hours

This is the curing window. The cyanoacrylate adhesive used in lash extensions takes a full day to reach maximum bond strength. Water exposure during this window weakens those bonds permanently — there's no recovery.

**What to avoid in the first 24 hours:**

- Showers with water directly on the face
- Swimming, hot springs, ocean
- Steam — hot yoga, sauna, steam room, opening the dishwasher
- Sweat from heavy exercise
- Makeup of any kind near the eye
- Crying (where possible)
- Sleeping face-down

**What is fine:**

- Drinking water normally
- Hand washing
- Eating normally (just don't lean over a steaming bowl of soup)
- Light walking and movement
- Working at a screen

If a client absolutely must exercise within 24 hours, keep heart rate low enough that sweat doesn't form. A short walk is fine; a hot yoga class is not.

## The first week

After the 24-hour cure, the daily-life rules begin. Most retention failures in the first week come from one of three culprits: oil-based skincare, makeup remover wipes, and waterproof mascara residue.

**Skincare to remove from the eye-area rotation:**

- Oil cleansers
- Micellar water (most formulas contain solvents that dissolve lash adhesive)
- Cleansing balms
- Eye creams with retinol or rich oil bases
- Makeup remover wipes (almost all use oil-based formulas to dissolve waterproof products)

**Skincare that's safe near the eyes:**

- Water-based foaming cleansers
- Gel cleansers labeled "oil-free"
- Lightweight, water-based moisturizers (applied below the brow bone, not the lash line)

**Makeup adjustments:**

- Mascara is unnecessary — the extensions already provide the mascara effect
- If mascara is genuinely needed for a specific event: water-based, lash-extension-safe formulas only, applied to the tips only, never the roots
- Eyeshadow is fine; powder formulas are safest; cream shadows with oil bases shorten retention
- Eyeliner against the lash line is fine; oil-based liner pencils dissolve adhesive

## Daily care

Two small habits make the difference between a set that ages gracefully and a set that looks ragged by week two.

**Clean once a day.** Foam-type lash shampoo is the standard. Apply with the soft brush provided, massage gently along the lash line (not just the tips), rinse with cool water, pat dry with a lint-free cloth. Cotton pads catch on extensions and pull them out.

The counterintuitive part: clean lashes last longer than dirty ones. Built-up skin oils, makeup residue, and dust weaken the adhesive bond over days. Daily cleaning prevents that buildup.

**Brush morning and evening.** The spoolie sent home from the appointment is for this. Gentle upward strokes from base to tip, separating any extensions that crossed during sleep. Twenty seconds. That's it.

**Pat dry, never rub.** After cleansing, after showering, after the rain — pat with a lint-free cloth. Rubbing is the single most damaging daily habit. It breaks the adhesive bond and pulls natural lashes out at the root.

## What to avoid completely

A short list of things that, in 7+ years of lash work, consistently halve a set's lifespan:

- **Oil-based cleansers, removers, and makeup wipes** — the single biggest retention killer
- **Waterproof mascara** — requires oil-based remover, which dissolves adhesive
- **Eyelash curlers** — bend the extensions out of shape and crack the bond
- **Rubbing the eyes**, especially when sleepy or itchy
- **Picking or pulling at the extensions** when they start to feel loose — let them shed naturally
- **Sauna, steam room, hot yoga in the first 48 hours** — moisture plus heat weakens fresh bonds
- **Sleeping face-down on the lashes for hours**

The sauna rule relaxes after the first 48 hours. After that, occasional steam exposure is fine — just rinse and pat dry afterward.

## How to sleep without losing lashes

Sleep position matters more than most clients expect. The pressure of a pillow against the lashes for 6–8 hours a night breaks bonds gradually, especially on the side a client favors.

**Back sleepers** retain best. By a wide margin.

**Side sleepers** lose lashes on the side they sleep on faster than the opposite side. By 3 weeks in, the asymmetry is visible.

**Stomach sleepers** lose lashes fastest of all. If switching positions isn't realistic, a silk pillowcase reduces friction enough to help.

A silk pillowcase is a worthwhile investment for any client who can't sleep on their back. It reduces friction, doesn't absorb skin oils the way cotton does, and extends retention by an estimated 20–30%.

## When to come in for a refill

Most clients refill every 2–3 weeks. Lighter styles (Classic 1:1, Light Volume) hold a bit longer and can stretch to 3–4 weeks. Heavier styles (Russian Volume, Mega Volume) need 2–3 weeks to look full.

**The sweet spot for a refill** is when the set has retained roughly 40–60% of the original extensions. Enough remains for the artist to map cleanly into the existing shape; not so few that the appointment becomes a near-rebuild.

**Signs you've waited too long:**

- More than 50% of the set has shed
- Visible gaps along the lash line
- Remaining extensions are growing out at angles that don't sit cleanly
- The set no longer looks like the original design

If it's been 4+ weeks, a refill often costs about the same as a fresh full set and looks worse afterward. Better to start fresh.
